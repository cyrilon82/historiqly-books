Executive Summary

In 1912, Charles Dawson and Arthur Smith Woodward announced a "missing link" called Eoanthropus dawsoni. For 41 years, the find shaped evolutionary narratives and academic orthodoxy. In 1953, fluorine dating, microscopy, and chemical analysis proved the specimens were fabricated: a medieval human cranium paired with a modern orangutan jaw, with filed teeth and artificial staining. A 2016 CT/DNA review confirmed a single, consistent forging methodology.

Key Findings

  • The specimens were fabricated from a medieval human skull and a modern orangutan jaw.
  • Teeth were manually filed to mimic human wear signatures.
  • Bones were chemically stained to imitate age and stratigraphic consistency.
  • 2016 high-resolution analyses indicate one coherent forger methodology across all items.
  • The total evidence profile points most strongly to Charles Dawson as principal forger.

Outstanding Uncertainties

  • Exact acquisition channel for orangutan remains is unknown.
  • Potential silent knowledge by contemporaries cannot be fully excluded.
  • No verified confession or supplier documentation has surfaced.
